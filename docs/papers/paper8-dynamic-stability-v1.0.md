# Dynamic Stability: A Measurement Architecture for Regulatory Collapse and Recovery

**Jani Halmetoja**

Preprint - 2026

---

## Abstract

This paper does not test whether representational stability varies across signal classes under activation. It proposes a measurement architecture through which such variation could be empirically investigated if it exists. The Structural Regulation Framework (SRF; Halmetoja, 2026a-g) proposes that psychological regulation operates through domain-specific compression of representational resolution under activation. However, these structural claims remain untestable without a corresponding measurement framework. The present paper introduces three complementary constructs - Compression Threshold (CT), Compression Curve (CC), and Dynamic Stability Profile (DSP) - as a minimal measurement architecture for operationalizing representational stability under load. CT identifies the activation intensity at which differentiated representation becomes unavailable. CC describes the shape of resolution degradation once compression begins. DSP combines CT and CC across signal classes into an integrated profile. A candidate pilot protocol for shame-domain compression measurement is proposed. The architecture is explicitly falsifiable: if CT produces no reliable individual differences, or if CC types cannot be distinguished empirically, the framework fails.

---

## 1. Introduction: The Measurement Gap

### 1.1 The Problem

Existing psychological measures largely assess what people report about themselves. The present paper proposes a framework for measuring how representational organization changes when psychologically significant activation occurs.

The Structural Regulation Framework (Halmetoja, 2026a) proposes that psychological regulation operates through signal-class-specific compression of representational resolution. Papers 1-7 of the SRF theory program develop this architecture across multiple layers: mechanism (Paper 1), change dynamics (Paper 2), clinical application (Paper 3), signal topology (Paper 4), vulnerability topology (Paper 5), existential regulation (Paper 6), and defensive reconstruction (Paper 7).

These papers make structural claims about how psychological systems behave under activation. However, structural claims without measurement architecture remain unfalsifiable. A theory that cannot specify how its constructs would appear in observable data cannot be empirically evaluated.

### 1.2 What This Paper Does

This paper proposes a measurement architecture - not empirical evidence. It asks:

> Can representational stability under activation be operationalized as a dynamic measurement architecture?

The paper does not claim that Compression Threshold exists as a stable individual-difference variable. It does not claim that Compression Curve types are empirically distinguishable. It does not claim that Dynamic Stability Profiles predict clinical outcomes.

It proposes: if these constructs exist, here is how they could be measured. Whether they exist is an empirical question that this paper enables but does not answer.

### 1.3 Scope

This is a measurement theory paper. It proposes constructs, operational definitions, and a candidate paradigm. It does not report data. Its value depends on whether the proposed architecture generates productive empirical investigation.

## 2. Background: Resolution Under Load

### 2.1 What Resolution Is Not

Within SRF, representational resolution refers to the capacity to maintain differentiated, multidimensional representations under activation without collapsing into simplified defensive encoding.

Resolution is not:
- **Insight:** A person may understand their patterns perfectly while remaining compressed under activation
- **Verbal complexity:** Articulate people can describe compressed experience in sophisticated language
- **Psychological knowledge:** Knowing about one's defenses does not prevent their operation

Resolution is: representational differentiation under load.

### 2.2 REL as Precedent

Regulation Externalization Latency (REL; Halmetoja, 2026a, REL paper) demonstrates that a single temporal parameter can be operationalized from SRF's theoretical architecture. REL measures when externalization occurs - one measurable aspect of regulatory behavior.

REL succeeded because it did not attempt to operationalize all of SRF simultaneously. It isolated one parameter, defined it precisely, proposed paradigms, and articulated discriminant validity arguments.

The present paper follows the same strategy for a different target: not when externalization occurs, but what happens to representational organization itself under activation.

### 2.3 The Missing Construct

REL measures behavioral output (externalization timing). But SRF's core claim concerns representational organization - the internal structure of how signals are processed. This internal dimension requires its own measurement approach.

The central question: at what point does representational differentiation fail, and how does it fail?

## 3. Compression Threshold (CT)

### 3.1 Definition

Compression Threshold (CT) is defined as:

> The activation intensity at which differentiated representation becomes unavailable within a specific signal class.

CT is a threshold parameter - it identifies the point at which the system transitions from differentiated to compressed processing.

### 3.2 Operational Specification

CT is operationalized as the activation level at which measurable indicators of representational differentiation fall below a predefined criterion.

Candidate indicators of differentiation:
- Number of distinct emotional states reported or expressed
- Capacity to generate multiple interpretations of an ambiguous stimulus
- Maintenance of contextual information alongside activated content
- Capacity to hold contradictory representations simultaneously
- Degree of self-other separation maintained under evaluative pressure

CT is identified as the activation level at which these indicators show significant decline from baseline.

### 3.3 Signal-Class Specificity

CT is hypothesized to vary across signal classes within individuals. A person may show:
- Low CT in shame domain (differentiation collapses under mild shame activation)
- High CT in professional domain (differentiation maintained under substantial performance pressure)

This intra-individual variation is a core prediction. If CT proves uniform across domains within individuals, the signal-class-specific architecture of SRF is not supported.

### 3.4 Relationship to Existing Constructs

**Distress tolerance** (Simons & Gaher, 2005): Measures capacity to endure negative affect. CT measures when representational differentiation fails - a different target. A person may tolerate high distress (high distress tolerance) while showing early differentiation collapse (low CT). These are conceptually independent.

**Window of tolerance** (Siegel, 1999): Describes the activation range within which a person can function adaptively. CT is more specific: it identifies the point at which representational differentiation specifically fails, not general adaptive functioning.

**Emotional reactivity:** Measures how strongly a person responds to emotional stimuli. CT measures when the quality of representation changes - not how intensely the person reacts.

## 4. Compression Curve (CC)

### 4.1 Definition

Compression Curve (CC) describes:

> The shape of representational resolution degradation as activation increases beyond Compression Threshold.

CT identifies where compression begins. CC describes how compression unfolds.

### 4.2 Candidate Types

Four candidate curve types are proposed:

**Type A - Linear Compression:** Resolution decreases gradually and proportionally as activation increases. No sudden transitions. Predictable degradation.

**Type B - Threshold Compression:** Resolution remains relatively stable until a critical point, then decreases rapidly. Apparent stability followed by sudden decline.

**Type C - Stepwise Compression:** Resolution decreases through discrete stages with identifiable intermediate states. Multiple thresholds produce layered collapse.

**Type D - Catastrophic Compression:** Resolution remains high until a narrow activation range is exceeded, then collapses almost entirely in a single transition. Minimal warning. Discontinuous state change.

### 4.3 Phase Transition Interpretation

Type D (Catastrophic Compression) may be best understood through phase transition dynamics rather than gradual degradation.

A phase transition is a qualitative state change rather than a quantitative shift along a continuum. The system operates in one state until a critical parameter is exceeded, then transitions discontinuously to a qualitatively different state.

If catastrophic compression operates as a phase transition:
- Warning signs may be structurally absent before the transition
- The system does not "weaken gradually" - it operates normally until it does not
- Recovery may require external stabilization rather than internal resources
- Traditional symptom monitoring cannot detect vulnerability because symptoms appear only after the transition

This interpretation draws on dynamical systems theory (Hollenstein, 2015; Thelen & Smith, 1994) and its application to emotional processes (Kuppens et al., 2010).

### 4.4 Relationship to Dynamical Systems Literature

The Compression Curve framework shares conceptual territory with several dynamical systems constructs:

**Attractors:** Stable representational states may function as attractors. Compression may represent transition between attractor basins - from a differentiated attractor to a compressed attractor.

**Tipping points:** CT may function as a tipping point - the parameter value at which the system transitions between qualitatively different states (Scheffer et al., 2009).

**Hysteresis:** Recovery from compression may not follow the same path as collapse. A system that collapses at activation level X may not recover until activation decreases to level Y < X. This asymmetry (hysteresis) is a testable prediction.

**Critical slowing down:** Before a phase transition, systems often show increased recovery time from perturbations (Scheffer et al., 2012). If catastrophic compression operates as a phase transition, critical slowing down may serve as an early warning indicator.

The present paper does not claim that psychological compression is formally equivalent to physical phase transitions. It proposes that phase transition dynamics offer a useful interpretive framework that generates testable predictions.

## 5. Dynamic Stability Profile (DSP)

### 5.1 Definition

Dynamic Stability Profile (DSP) is defined as:

> The combination of CT and CC across multiple signal classes for an individual.

DSP represents how the system behaves under activation - not what the system reports about itself.

### 5.2 Example Profiles

The following profiles are presented as illustrative examples of how CT and CC parameters might combine - not as proposed categories or a typology. DSP is a continuous, multidimensional profile space. These examples highlight qualitatively different regions of that space to clarify the framework's clinical implications.

**Profile A - Stable Integration:**
- High CT across domains
- Linear CC
- Characteristics: resilient, gradual degradation, high adaptability

**Profile B - Hidden Fragility:**
- High CT across domains
- Catastrophic CC in specific domains
- Characteristics: appears highly stable, sudden collapse risk, low warning visibility
- Clinical relevance: narcissistic collapse, identity-level crises, "I never would have expected them to collapse"

**Profile C - Rapid Externalization:**
- Low CT across domains
- Linear CC
- Characteristics: predictable instability, visible distress, low containment

**Profile D - Volatile Collapse:**
- Low CT across domains
- Catastrophic CC
- Characteristics: rapid dysregulation, severe instability, high collapse probability

### 5.3 Clinical Utility

DSP may reveal information that symptom-based assessment cannot:

- **Profile B is invisible to symptom measures.** The system produces no symptoms until collapse occurs. Only DSP (specifically: high CT + catastrophic CC) would predict this vulnerability.
- **Profiles C and D look similar on symptom measures** (both show distress) but differ in collapse dynamics. Treatment implications may differ.
- **Longitudinal DSP tracking** may detect structural change that precedes symptom change.

### 5.4 Profile B and Phase Transition

Profile B deserves special attention because it describes a clinically important phenomenon that traditional assessment cannot detect: the person who appears stable until they suddenly are not.

Traditional assessment monitors symptoms. Profile B produces no symptoms until the phase transition occurs. By the time symptoms appear, collapse has already happened.

DSP assessment would identify Profile B vulnerability before collapse - not by detecting symptoms, but by characterizing the system's collapse dynamics under controlled activation.

## 6. Relationship to Existing Literature

### 6.1 Emotional Granularity

Emotional granularity (Kashdan et al., 2015; Barrett, 2006) refers to the precision with which individuals differentiate their emotional experiences. Higher granularity is associated with better emotion regulation and psychological health.

DSF's relationship to granularity:
- **Convergence:** Both concern differentiation of emotional experience
- **Divergence:** Granularity is typically measured at rest or in retrospective self-report. DSF measures differentiation specifically under activation load. A person may show high granularity in calm self-report while showing rapid differentiation collapse under activation.
- **Extension:** DSF proposes that granularity is not a fixed trait but a dynamic parameter that degrades under load - and that the pattern of degradation (CC) carries clinically important information.

### 6.2 Levels of Emotional Awareness

The Levels of Emotional Awareness Scale (LEAS; Lane et al., 1990) measures the complexity and differentiation of emotional awareness across developmental levels.

DSF's relationship to LEAS:
- **Convergence:** Both concern representational complexity of emotional experience
- **Divergence:** LEAS measures the level of awareness achievable. DSF measures the persistence of awareness under activation. A person may achieve high LEAS scores (capable of complex emotional awareness) while showing low CT (awareness collapses rapidly under activation).
- **Prediction:** LEAS and CT should be partially independent. High LEAS + low CT = "knows but cannot maintain under pressure."

### 6.3 Affect Dynamics

Affect dynamics research (Kuppens et al., 2010; Houben et al., 2015) examines temporal patterns in emotional experience: variability, inertia, instability, and recovery.

This is the closest existing literature to DSF:
- **Convergence:** Both concern dynamic properties of emotional systems rather than static traits
- **Divergence:** Affect dynamics typically examines natural fluctuation over time. DSF examines response to controlled activation escalation. Affect dynamics measures what happens spontaneously. DSF measures what happens under load.
- **Shared constructs:** Emotional inertia (resistance to change) may relate to Resolution Resistance (Paper 2). Recovery time may relate to CT (systems with lower CT may show longer recovery).

### 6.4 Dynamical Systems Approaches

Dynamical systems theory applied to emotion and development (Hollenstein, 2015; Thelen & Smith, 1994; Lewis, 2005) provides the most natural theoretical home for DSF.

Key shared concepts:
- **State space:** DSP can be conceptualized as a description of the system's state space under activation
- **Attractors:** Differentiated and compressed states as competing attractors
- **Bifurcations:** CT as a bifurcation point where the system transitions between attractor basins
- **Phase transitions:** Catastrophic CC as a first-order phase transition
- **Hysteresis:** Asymmetry between collapse and recovery paths

DSF extends dynamical systems approaches by proposing:
- Signal-class specificity (different dynamics in different domains within the same person)
- Explicit threshold and curve-shape parameters
- Integration into a profile (DSP) that characterizes the full system

### 6.5 Explicit Novelty Claim

The proposed novelty of DSF lies in:

1. **Signal-class-specific stability measurement** - not global resilience but domain-specific collapse dynamics
2. **Threshold + curve shape as integrated parameters** - CT alone is insufficient; CC adds collapse architecture
3. **Profile-level characterization** (DSP) - combining parameters across domains into a clinically interpretable profile
4. **Phase transition interpretation** of catastrophic compression - connecting psychological collapse to dynamical systems theory
5. **Measurement under controlled escalation** - not retrospective report or natural fluctuation, but systematic activation increase

## 7. Candidate Pilot Protocol: Shame Compression

### 7.1 Rationale for Shame Domain

Shame is selected as the pilot domain because:
- Shame activation is reliably inducible in laboratory settings
- Shame shows high clinical relevance across multiple presentations
- Shame compression is hypothesized to show high variability across individuals
- Shame-related differentiation collapse is clinically observable and recognizable

### 7.2 Baseline Condition

Participants complete a differentiation assessment under neutral conditions:
- Describe a recent mildly positive social interaction
- Rate emotional complexity (multiple emotions identified)
- Generate multiple interpretations of an ambiguous social vignette
- Demonstrate self-other separation in a neutral evaluative scenario

This establishes baseline differentiation capacity without activation load.

### 7.3 Activation Condition

Shame activation is introduced through guided autobiographical recall:
- Participant identifies a shame-relevant personal memory (screened for moderate intensity)
- Guided imagery protocol brings the memory into present-tense experiential focus
- Activation is confirmed through self-report and physiological indicators (optional: skin conductance, heart rate)

### 7.4 Escalation Condition

Activation intensity is progressively increased through:
- Deepening of imagery (sensory detail, interpersonal context)
- Introduction of observer perspective ("imagine others watching")
- Amplification of evaluative component ("what does this say about you?")
- Progressive removal of contextual buffers

Escalation proceeds through predefined steps (minimum 5-7 levels) to allow curve-shape identification.

### 7.5 Measurement: Differentiation Coding

At each escalation level, differentiation is assessed through:
- **Emotion differentiation:** Number of distinct emotions reported (coded from open-ended response)
- **Interpretation multiplicity:** Number of viable interpretations generated for a standardized probe
- **Context maintenance:** Degree to which broader life context remains accessible (coded from response)
- **Self-other separation:** Degree to which self-evaluation remains distinct from the specific event

A composite differentiation score is computed at each level.

### 7.6 CT Identification

CT is identified as the escalation level at which the composite differentiation score drops below a predefined criterion (e.g., 50% of baseline, or statistically significant decline from baseline using within-subject comparison).

### 7.7 CC Identification

CC type is classified based on the shape of the differentiation-by-activation function:
- **Linear:** Approximately constant slope across levels
- **Threshold:** Stable followed by rapid decline (identified by breakpoint analysis)
- **Stepwise:** Multiple discrete drops (identified by step-function fitting)
- **Catastrophic:** Single large drop within one escalation step (identified by maximum single-step decline exceeding predefined criterion)

Classification may use visual inspection, breakpoint regression, or model comparison (linear vs. step-function vs. threshold models).

### 7.8 Proposed Sample, Design, Analysis

**Sample:** N = 30-50 (sufficient for individual-difference detection and CC type classification)

**Design:** Within-subject escalation (all participants complete all levels). Between-subject comparison of CT values and CC types.

**Analysis:**
- CT: Mean and variance of threshold values. Test whether CT shows reliable individual differences (ICC from repeated measurement).
- CC: Classification of curve types. Test whether types are distinguishable (model comparison). Test whether classification is reliable (inter-rater agreement for visual classification; model fit indices for statistical classification).
- Validity: Correlate CT with existing measures (distress tolerance, emotional granularity, LEAS) to establish convergent and discriminant validity.

### 7.9 Reliability Assessment

A critical question for any measurement architecture: can results be reproduced?

**Test-retest reliability of CT:** Administer the protocol twice (separated by 2-4 weeks). Compute ICC for CT values. If CT shows poor test-retest reliability, it may reflect state variation rather than stable individual differences - which would require reconceptualization.

**CC classification stability:** Assess whether the same individual receives the same CC type classification across repeated administrations. If CC type is unstable, it may be state-dependent rather than architecturally determined.

**Inter-rater reliability:** For differentiation coding and CC classification, compute inter-rater agreement (Cohen's kappa or ICC depending on measurement level).

Acceptable reliability is a prerequisite for all subsequent validity claims. If the architecture cannot produce reliable measurements, it fails regardless of theoretical coherence.

## 8. Predictions

### 8.1 CT Varies Across Signal Classes Within Individuals

If SRF's signal-class-specific architecture is correct, the same individual should show different CT values across different signal domains. A person with high shame compression should show lower CT in shame-relevant activation than in status-relevant activation.

**Test:** Administer escalation protocols across multiple signal classes within the same participants. Compute within-person variance in CT across domains. If CT is uniform across domains, signal-class specificity is not supported.

### 8.2 CC Type Correlates With Clinical Presentation

If CC types are clinically meaningful, they should correlate with observable clinical patterns:
- Catastrophic CC should correlate with history of sudden collapse events
- Stepwise CC should correlate with gradual deterioration patterns
- Linear CC should correlate with predictable, proportionate stress responses

**Test:** Assess CC type and correlate with clinical history (retrospective report of collapse patterns, therapist ratings).

### 8.3 Profile B Predicts Sudden Collapse Events

If Profile B (High CT + Catastrophic CC) represents hidden fragility, individuals with this profile should show higher rates of sudden, unexpected collapse events compared to other profiles.

**Test:** Prospective study. Assess DSP at baseline. Track collapse events over follow-up period. Profile B should predict sudden collapse events beyond what symptom measures predict.

### 8.4 Ontological Coupling Hypothesis

If the Ontological Coupling Hypothesis is correct, signal classes with higher self-architecture stakes should show more catastrophic CC types.

**Test:** Assess self-architecture stakes per signal class (Paper 5 dimensions). Assess CC type per signal class. Higher stakes should predict more catastrophic curves.

## 9. Future Research Directions

### 9.1 CC Shape Change as Therapeutic Progress Marker

If therapeutic change involves modification of collapse dynamics, CC shape may change across therapy (e.g., catastrophic to stepwise). This would require longitudinal DSP assessment at multiple therapy timepoints.

This prediction requires substantial longitudinal data and is proposed as a future research direction rather than a near-term testable hypothesis.

### 9.2 Multi-Domain DSP Assessment

The pilot protocol proposes single-domain assessment (shame). Full DSP requires assessment across multiple signal classes. Future work should develop parallel protocols for dependency, rejection, status, and control domains.

### 9.3 Physiological Correlates

CT and CC transitions may have physiological signatures (heart rate variability changes, skin conductance responses, cortisol patterns). Integration of physiological measurement could provide objective activation markers and reduce reliance on self-report escalation.

### 9.4 Cross-Cultural Stability

Whether CT, CC, and DSP show similar properties across cultural contexts is unknown. Shame activation, in particular, may operate differently across cultures with different shame norms.

## 10. Limitations

### 10.1 No Empirical Data

This paper proposes a measurement architecture. No data has been collected. All constructs, predictions, and paradigms are theoretical proposals requiring empirical testing. The framework may prove unmeasurable, unreliable, or uninformative upon contact with data.

### 10.2 Activation Standardization

"Shame activation" means different things to different people. Standardized escalation protocols may produce different activation intensities across individuals, confounding CT measurement. Individual calibration (using personally relevant material) increases ecological validity but reduces standardization.

### 10.3 Verbal Ability Confound

Differentiation coding relies partly on verbal output. Individuals with higher verbal ability may produce more differentiated responses regardless of actual representational state. Controls for verbal intelligence are necessary but may not fully eliminate this confound.

### 10.4 Ecological Validity

Laboratory escalation may not reproduce the dynamics of real-world activation. A person who shows high CT in the laboratory may show low CT in actual relational conflict. The relationship between laboratory-measured and naturalistic CT is an empirical question.

### 10.5 CC Type Identification

Reliable CC classification requires sufficient escalation steps to distinguish curve shapes. If escalation is too coarse (too few steps), linear and threshold curves may be indistinguishable. The minimum number of escalation steps required for reliable classification is unknown.

### 10.6 Reliability Unknown

Whether CT and CC produce reliable measurements across repeated administrations is entirely unknown. If these parameters show poor test-retest reliability, the architecture may need fundamental reconceptualization (e.g., as state-dependent rather than architecturally stable parameters).

---

## 11. Conclusion

The Structural Regulation Framework proposes that psychological regulation operates through domain-specific compression of representational resolution under activation. If this is correct, representational stability should be measurable.

This paper proposes a minimal measurement architecture consisting of three constructs:
- **Compression Threshold (CT):** where differentiation fails
- **Compression Curve (CC):** how differentiation fails
- **Dynamic Stability Profile (DSP):** the integrated pattern across signal classes

The architecture is explicitly falsifiable. If CT shows no reliable individual differences, the construct fails. If CC types cannot be empirically distinguished, the typology fails. If DSP adds no predictive value beyond existing measures, the profile fails.

One candidate pilot protocol (shame-domain compression) is proposed as sufficient to establish initial feasibility. The protocol is designed to answer the most basic question: can representational stability under activation be measured at all?

This paper does not claim that these constructs exist. It proposes how they could be measured if they do. Whether they exist - whether representational stability degrades in measurable, classifiable, signal-class-specific patterns under activation - is an empirical question that this architecture enables but does not answer.

# Compression Curve Theory

## A Proposed Dynamic Extension of Compression Threshold Within SRF

### Status

Conceptual note.

Not empirically validated.

Proposed as a candidate extension to Resolution Measurement Framework (RMF).

---

# Core Question

Compression Threshold (CT) asks:

> At what activation intensity does differentiated representation become unavailable?

This is useful.

However, a threshold alone does not describe how compression unfolds.

Two individuals may have identical thresholds while exhibiting radically different compression dynamics.

Therefore:

> Compression may be better understood as a curve rather than a single point.

---

# Core Definition

Compression Curve (CC) describes the relationship between:

- activation intensity
- available representational resolution

within a specific signal class.

The curve answers:

> How does resolution change as activation increases?

---

# Why Compression Curves Matter

Compression Threshold identifies where failure begins.

Compression Curve describes how failure unfolds.

This distinction is analogous to engineering.

Knowing the breaking point of a material is useful.

Knowing how the material behaves under increasing load is often more informative.

---

# Candidate Compression Patterns

## Type A: Linear Compression

Resolution gradually decreases as activation increases.

Example:

Activation increases. Resolution decreases proportionally. No sudden collapse occurs.

Characteristics:
- predictable
- gradual deterioration
- stable adaptation

Possible example domains:
- mild performance anxiety
- moderate uncertainty

---

## Type B: Threshold Compression

Resolution remains relatively stable until a critical point is reached.

After crossing the threshold: resolution rapidly decreases.

Characteristics:
- apparent stability
- sudden deterioration
- threshold-sensitive

Possible example domains:
- abandonment fears
- attachment activation

---

## Type C: Stepwise Compression

Resolution decreases through discrete stages.

Example:

High differentiation -> Partial category collapse -> Global category collapse -> Highly compressed dichotomous processing

Characteristics:
- multiple thresholds
- layered collapse
- hierarchical compression

Potential relevance:
- complex developmental trauma
- personality pathology

---

## Type D: Catastrophic Compression

Resolution remains high until a narrow activation range is exceeded.

Then: large-scale collapse occurs almost immediately.

Characteristics:
- dramatic state change
- low warning visibility
- high instability after collapse

Potential relevance:
- narcissistic collapse
- severe shame activation
- identity-level threats

---

# Compression Stages

Compression may unfold through multiple levels.

---

## Stage 1: Differentiated Representation

Multiple emotions, interpretations, and contextual factors remain accessible.

Example:

- shame
- sadness
- fear
- disappointment
- dependency
- uncertainty

remain distinct.

---

## Stage 2: Partial Compression

Previously differentiated signals begin to merge.

Example:

- shame and fear merge
- disappointment and sadness merge

Resolution decreases. Context remains available.

---

## Stage 3: Categorical Compression

Complex representations collapse into simplified interpretations.

Examples:

- "I am failing."
- "Nobody cares."
- "Everything is ruined."

Context becomes increasingly inaccessible.

---

## Stage 4: Highly Compressed Dichotomous Processing

Representations collapse into oppositional categories.

Examples:

- success / failure
- acceptance / rejection
- worthy / worthless

Nuance largely disappears.

---

## Stage 5: Ontological Binary Encoding

The deepest collapse state.

Representational organization becomes existential.

Examples:

- continuity of being / annihilation
- safe / destroyed
- self-preserved / self-dissolved

This stage is proposed as an extreme state rather than a normal operating condition.

---

# Signal-Class Specificity

Compression Curves may differ across signal classes.

An individual may display:

| Signal Class | Curve Type |
|---|---|
| Shame | Catastrophic |
| Dependency | Stepwise |
| Status | Linear |
| Ambiguity | Threshold |
| Rejection | Catastrophic |

This produces a dynamic Compression Profile.

---

# Relationship to Compression Threshold

Compression Threshold and Compression Curve describe different aspects of the same process.

Compression Threshold:

> Where does compression begin?

Compression Curve:

> How does compression unfold?

Both may be required for a complete description of representational stability.

---

# Relationship to RMF

RMF measures resolution under activation.

Compression Curve extends RMF by describing the shape of resolution loss.

CT provides:
- location

CC provides:
- dynamics

Together they create a richer model of domain-specific compression.

# Candidate Prediction

Individuals with similar symptom profiles may possess different Compression Curves.

Likewise:

Two individuals with identical Compression Thresholds may exhibit radically different collapse dynamics.

Therefore:

Curve shape may contain clinically important information not captured by threshold alone.

---

# Foundational Hypothesis

Psychological functioning may be characterized not only by how much activation a system can tolerate before compression begins, but also by how representational resolution degrades once compression starts.

If Compression Threshold identifies vulnerability,

Compression Curve identifies collapse architecture.

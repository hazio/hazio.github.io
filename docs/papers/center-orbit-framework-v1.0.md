# CENTER/ORBIT Framework
## A Structural Model of Internalized and Externalized Regulation

**Version 1.0**

---

## Abstract

The CENTER/ORBIT Framework proposes a structural model of human psychological regulation organized around the location of emotional stabilization. The framework suggests that many psychological patterns traditionally described as personality traits, attachment styles, or interpersonal dynamics may reflect differences in where regulation is primarily located: internally maintained (CENTER) or externally maintained (ORBIT).

The model integrates concepts from attachment theory, affect regulation, self psychology, object relations theory, nervous system regulation, and behavioral genetics into a unified structural framework. Rather than classifying individuals categorically, the model proposes a dimensional axis describing the degree to which emotional stability, identity continuity, and tension regulation depend on external relational structures.

A central construct of the framework is Regulation Externalization Latency (REL): the time interval between unresolved emotional activation and initiation of external regulatory behavior. REL is proposed as a dynamic behavioral indicator that may capture variance in regulation organization not fully measured by existing attachment, distress tolerance, or differentiation constructs.

The framework distinguishes between behavioral stability and structural integration, external regulation and internalized regulation, and temporary stabilization and long-term coherence. It adopts a probabilistic developmental model in which regulation organization emerges through interaction between genetic predisposition, temperament, nervous system sensitivity, and individual-specific environmental experiences — consistent with recent behavioral genetic evidence showing that familial environmental factors shared by siblings play a negligible role in the development of narcissism-related traits (Back et al., 2026).

This paper introduces the conceptual structure of the model, proposes operational definitions for its primary constructs, outlines preliminary hypotheses for future empirical testing, and acknowledges substantial limitations requiring empirical validation.

# 1. Introduction

## 1.1 The Central Problem

Many existing psychological frameworks describe symptoms, attachment behaviors, personality patterns, emotional dysregulation, and relational instability without fully modeling the structural location of psychological stability itself.

The CENTER/ORBIT Framework proposes that one of the primary differentiators between psychological structures is where emotional coherence is primarily maintained. The model suggests that many apparently different psychological patterns may represent variations of externalized regulation strategies.

## 1.2 A Structural Rather Than Moral Model

The framework does not attempt to classify individuals as healthy or unhealthy, good or bad, strong or weak. Instead, the model interprets psychological behavior structurally. Many behaviors commonly interpreted morally may instead reflect adaptive attempts to regulate unresolved tension, preserve attachment, or maintain psychological continuity.

## 1.3 Distinguishing Stability From Integration

One of the primary aims of the model is to distinguish between external behavioral stability and internal structural integration. A person may appear calm, controlled, successful, and relationally stable while still depending heavily on external regulation for psychological coherence. Conversely, emotionally intense or relationally sensitive individuals may possess greater internal authenticity while lacking stable regulation capacity.

## 1.4 Regulation as a Dynamic Process

The framework conceptualizes regulation as dynamic rather than categorical. Individuals are not assumed to exist permanently in either CENTER or ORBIT. Instead, the model proposes that all psychological systems shift along a CENTER–ORBIT axis depending on developmental history, attachment activation, stress load, uncertainty, relational threat, nervous system capacity, and environmental stability.

## 1.5 Operational Goals

A central goal of the framework is operationalization. The model aims to develop measurable constructs capable of empirical investigation, including CENTER capacity, external regulation dependence, tension retention, reassurance-seeking latency, self-continuity under relational stress, and authenticity preservation during attachment activation.

## 1.6 Dynamic Process Contribution

The framework proposes that its primary incremental contribution lies not in static personality description alone, but in modeling regulation direction, regulation timing, and externalization dynamics under unresolved tension. Specifically, the construct of Regulation Externalization Latency (REL) — how rapidly unresolved activation externalizes into relational regulation behavior — is proposed as a dynamic process variable not fully captured by existing attachment, distress tolerance, or differentiation measures.

# 2. Core Definitions

## 2.1 Regulation

Regulation refers to the process through which a psychological system maintains emotional, physiological, and relational stability under conditions of tension, uncertainty, or activation. Regulation may occur internally, externally, or through combinations of both. The critical distinction is not the existence of external regulation, but the degree of structural dependence upon it.

## 2.2 Internalized Regulation (CENTER)

CENTER refers to the structural capacity to maintain internal coherence under unresolved emotional or relational tension. CENTER is not defined by emotional suppression, independence, stoicism, or social withdrawal. Instead, CENTER describes the ability to remain psychologically continuous, tolerate uncertainty, sustain authenticity during relational stress, and remain connected without identity destabilization. CENTER is conceptualized as a capacity rather than a permanent state.

## 2.3 Externalized Regulation (ORBIT)

ORBIT refers to a mode of regulation in which psychological stability becomes increasingly dependent on external relational systems. ORBIT dynamics emerge when unresolved tension is rapidly externalized into attachment, mirroring, reassurance seeking, symbolic dependence, emotional fusion, or relational monitoring. ORBIT is not conceptualized as pathology in itself. The framework assumes that all humans shift between CENTER and ORBIT states depending on context, stress load, developmental history, and regulation capacity.

## 2.4 Tension

Tension refers to unresolved activation requiring regulatory processing.

For conceptual clarity, the framework distinguishes several subclasses of tension:

| Tension subclass | Description | Typical triggers |
|---|---|---|
| **Attachment tension** | Unresolved relational uncertainty, separation threat, abandonment activation | Delayed response, withdrawal, ambiguous availability |
| **Identity tension** | Instability in self-coherence, evaluative threat, self-concept disruption | Criticism, failure, contradictory feedback |
| **Contradiction tension** | Simultaneous incompatible realities requiring integration | Ambivalence, moral conflict, relational paradox |
| **Shame tension** | Internally destabilizing self-evaluative affect | Exposure, perceived inadequacy, public error |
| **Control tension** | Uncertainty generated by unpredictability or loss of environmental control | Chaos, powerlessness, uncontrollable outcomes |

These subclasses are not mutually exclusive. Multiple tension types may co-activate simultaneously.

The handling of unresolved tension — regardless of subclass — is proposed as one of the primary differentiators between CENTER and ORBIT regulation strategies.

The framework further proposes that ORBIT vectors may show preferential (though not exclusive) responsiveness to specific tension subclasses:
- Validation vector → may be preferentially activated by identity tension
- Dominance vector → may be preferentially activated by control tension
- Shame-defense vector → may be preferentially activated by shame tension

These correspondences are proposed as tendencies, not fixed mappings. In practice, multiple tension types may activate multiple vectors simultaneously, and individuals may externalize through vectors that do not correspond to the primary tension subclass. This correspondence remains hypothetical and requires empirical testing.

## 2.5 Integration

Integration refers to the capacity of the psychological system to maintain coherent continuity across differing emotional states, relational contexts, and internal contradictions. The model distinguishes integration from surface-level behavioral stability. A system may appear stable externally while remaining structurally dependent on external regulation.

## 2.6 Mirroring

Mirroring refers to external reflective input that alters perceived self-coherence, identity stability, or emotional legitimacy.

Operational distinction: ordinary social feedback informs behavior; mirroring modifies self-state stability. A comment that changes what someone does is feedback. A comment that changes how someone experiences themselves is mirroring.

The framework proposes that mirroring functions as a dynamic regulation system operating across all significant relationships throughout the lifespan — not exclusively through early parental relationships. Mirror dependence refers to the degree to which self-state stability requires ongoing mirroring input.

## 2.7 Regulation Externalization Latency (REL)

REL refers to the time interval between unresolved emotional or relational activation and initiation of external regulatory behavior. REL is proposed as a dynamic behavioral indicator of regulation organization, measuring how rapidly distress transforms into external regulation behavior rather than merely whether distress exists.

# 3. Theoretical Background

The CENTER/ORBIT Framework is intended as a structural synthesis integrating concepts from multiple existing traditions into a unified regulation-based model.

## 3.1 Attachment Theory

The framework builds upon attachment theory (Bowlby; Ainsworth), proposing that attachment patterns may partially reflect differing degrees of internalized versus externalized regulation. Anxious attachment may involve rapid externalization of tension into relational monitoring; avoidant attachment may involve defensive suppression of external regulation needs; secure attachment may reflect greater capacity for internally maintained coherence.

## 3.2 Affect Regulation Theory

Drawing from Schore's affect regulation research, the framework adopts the assumption that regulation capacity develops relationally before becoming internalized. Disturbances in this internalization process may contribute to persistent dependence on external regulatory structures.

## 3.3 Self Psychology

Incorporating Kohut's self psychology, the framework extends mirroring from a primarily developmental phenomenon to a dynamic regulation system. The concepts of mirror dependence, high-value mirror nodes, and mirror economy represent attempts to formalize how external reflective structures stabilize identity and emotional continuity.

## 3.4 Object Relations Theory

Drawing from Winnicott, Klein, and Kernberg, the framework extends object relations concepts by proposing that unresolved relational tension influences whether regulation remains internally organized or externally distributed. Winnicott's true self/false self distinction strongly influences the framework's treatment of authenticity and identity adaptation.

## 3.5 Nervous System Regulation

The framework incorporates concepts from autonomic regulation research, including polyvagal theory (Porges), while proposing that psychological regulation is inseparable from physiological regulation. Unresolved tension is conceptualized not merely cognitively but as nervous system activation and unresolved autonomic arousal.

## 3.6 Behavioral Genetics and Gene–Environment Interaction

Recent behavioral genetic research has demonstrated that individual differences in narcissism-related traits are substantially heritable (approximately 50%), with negligible contribution from shared familial environments (Back et al., 2026). This finding challenges developmental models that place primary emphasis on parenting style as the origin of narcissistic or externalized regulation structures.

The framework therefore adopts a probabilistic developmental position: regulation organization emerges through dynamic interaction between genetic predisposition, temperament, nervous system sensitivity, and individual-specific environmental experiences. The framework explicitly rejects simplistic parent-blame models and single-cause developmental explanations.

Critically, the 50% of variance attributable to individual-specific (nonshared) environmental factors — including experiences with peers, romantic partners, and occupational contexts — aligns with the framework's emphasis on relational regulation dynamics as ongoing processes throughout the lifespan, not merely products of early childhood.

The framework further notes that genetic predisposition toward narcissism-related traits may partially reflect biologically influenced differences in reward sensitivity, approach motivation, and dopaminergic responsiveness (DeYoung, 2013; Zajenkowski et al., 2023), consistent with the framework's proposed concept of externally weighted regulation organization.

## 3.7 Systems Theory

The framework is influenced by systems-oriented thinking, conceptualizing the psyche as a dynamic regulation system organized around tension management and stability preservation rather than a collection of independent symptoms.

## 3.8 Positioning Relative to Bowen's Differentiation of Self

The framework acknowledges strong conceptual overlap with Murray Bowen's differentiation of self theory. Bowen described differentiation as the capacity to maintain psychological self-definition while remaining emotionally connected within relational systems. The framework attempts to extend differentiation theory by focusing specifically on regulation direction under unresolved emotional tension, and by introducing time-sensitive regulatory dynamics (REL) as a core investigational domain not captured by existing differentiation measures.

## 3.9 Incremental Contribution

The framework proposes that its primary contribution involves the dynamic direction and timing of regulation externalization under unresolved relational tension. Existing constructs (attachment anxiety, differentiation of self, distress tolerance, self-concept clarity) primarily describe relational style, emotional tolerance, identity coherence, or subjective stability. The framework proposes that these constructs do not fully capture how rapidly unresolved activation externalizes into relational regulation behavior.

Specifically, the framework predicts that CENTER capacity may explain variance in reassurance-seeking latency, authenticity preservation, identity continuity under attachment activation, mirror dependence, and relational externalization patterns above and beyond existing measures. This claim remains hypothetical and requires empirical testing.

# 4. The CENTER–ORBIT Axis

## 4.1 Dimensional Rather Than Categorical

The framework conceptualizes psychological regulation along a dynamic continuum. CENTER describes systems capable of maintaining internal coherence under unresolved tension. ORBIT describes systems increasingly dependent on external relational structures for stabilization. The distinction is dimensional rather than categorical.

## 4.2 CENTER Is Not Emotional Flatness

The framework distinguishes between genuine integration and defensive suppression. A person may appear externally calm while maintaining stability through emotional shutdown, rigid control, dissociation, or compulsive self-sufficiency. The framework does not classify such patterns as CENTER if coherence depends on defensive restriction rather than integrated regulation capacity.

## 4.3 Pseudo-CENTER and Suppression-Control

Pseudo-CENTER refers to externally stable functioning maintained through defensive restriction rather than integrated regulation. The framework introduces a suppression-control variable to distinguish integrated CENTER capacity from defensive pseudo-CENTER organization.

Proposed suppression-control indicators include:
- "I stay emotionally stable by shutting down what I feel."
- "When emotional intensity rises, I detach rather than process."
- "My stability depends on remaining emotionally controlled."

The framework predicts that integrated CENTER capacity should correlate with emotional flexibility and authenticity stability rather than emotional suppression alone.

## 4.4 Structural Levels

The framework distinguishes four levels of regulatory organization to resolve the state-trait ambiguity:

| Level | Description | Temporal scale | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **State** | Temporary activation state | Seconds to hours | Momentary ORBIT activation during argument |
| **Strategy** | Preferred regulatory response | Days to weeks | Habitual reassurance-seeking after conflict |
| **Architecture** | Habitual organization | Months to years | Chronic external regulation dependence |
| **Capacity** | Maximum self-regulation competence | Developmental | Ability to hold tension under optimal conditions |

These levels relate to framework constructs as follows:

- **REL** operates at the state/strategy level — it measures momentary externalization speed
- **ORBIT vectors** operate at the strategy level — they describe preferred regulatory channels
- **Chronic ORBIT** operates at the architecture level — it describes persistent structural organization
- **CENTER capacity** operates at the capacity level — it describes maximum available internal regulation

This hierarchy clarifies that:
- A person with high CENTER capacity may still enter ORBIT states under sufficient activation
- A person with chronic ORBIT architecture may still demonstrate CENTER-like behavior in low-stress contexts
- Strategy-level patterns may shift faster than architecture-level organization
- Capacity represents the ceiling, not the typical operating point

The framework proposes that therapeutic change primarily targets architecture and capacity levels, while state and strategy levels fluctuate naturally with context.

Note: The terminology CENTER and ORBIT is mnemonic rather than normative. CENTER does not imply superiority, and ORBIT does not imply pathology. The terms describe regulatory location, not human value.

## 4.5 Chronic ORBIT

Chronic ORBIT refers to persistent structural dependence on external regulation systems for maintaining coherence. Temporary ORBIT activation is assumed to be a universal human experience under sufficient stress. Chronic ORBIT refers instead to persistent structural inability to maintain coherence without external stabilization.

## 4.6 Regulation Direction

The framework proposes that unresolved tension may move in multiple directions:
- **Internal processing**: activation remains internally metabolized temporarily
- **Defensive suppression**: activation becomes inhibited or emotionally restricted
- **External regulation**: activation rapidly externalizes into reassurance-seeking, relational monitoring, mirroring dependence, or symbolic attachment

## 4.7 ORBIT Vectors

The framework proposes that ORBIT is not a single phenomenon but a multidirectional regulatory space. ORBIT describes the general direction of regulation (externalized). Vectors describe the specific channel or motive through which externalization occurs.

The vector concept is proposed as illustrative rather than exhaustive. The following three vectors are presented as canonical exemplars to demonstrate the structural principle. Additional vectors (e.g., attachment, idealization, symbolic stabilization) may exist and are noted as potential future extensions.

### Definition

An individual's externalized regulation may proceed through different channels depending on context, activation type, and developmental history. Rather than proposing fixed subtypes, the framework describes regulatory vectors — directions of externalization that may combine, shift, and vary in intensity.

### Three Canonical Vectors

| Vector | Mechanism | Characteristic regulatory logic | Stabilization through |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Validation** | tension → insecurity → seek reflection → temporary stabilization | "Stability requires external confirmation" | External approval, admiration, recognition |
| **Dominance** | tension → threat perception → control acquisition → stabilization | "Stability requires environmental predictability" | Predictability, hierarchy, power |
| **Shame-defense** | tension → shame activation → self-protection → external distortion | "Uncontained shame becomes destabilizing" | Projection, blame-shifting, devaluation, splitting |

These three are proposed because they represent structurally distinct regulatory mechanisms (seeking input vs. controlling environment vs. expelling internal state) and because they correspond to well-documented clinical phenomena.

### Additional Vectors (Future Extensions)

The following vectors are noted as conceptually plausible but require further theoretical development and empirical investigation before formal inclusion:

- **Attachment**: stability through proximity and relational fusion
- **Idealization**: stability through symbolic amplification of an external object
- **Symbolic**: stability through meaning systems, ideology, or group identity

### Why Vectors Rather Than Subtypes

The framework explicitly rejects categorical subtyping (e.g., "ORBIT-A", "ORBIT-B") for the following reasons:

1. **Individuals may operate through multiple vectors simultaneously.** A person may externalize through validation-seeking in one context and shame-defense in another.

2. **Vectors shift dynamically.** The same individual may shift between vectors depending on stress load, relational context, and activation level.

3. **Avoids typological degeneration.** Fixed subtypes risk recreating DSM-style personality categories — precisely what the framework aims to avoid.

4. **Preserves dimensional structure.** Each vector can vary in intensity independently, creating a multidimensional regulatory profile rather than a categorical label.

### Possible Structural Interpretations

The vector model may offer structural reinterpretation of existing clinical and personality constructs. These interpretations remain speculative and require empirical validation:

- Vulnerable narcissism may be structurally interpretable as externalization primarily through validation and shame-defense vectors
- Grandiose narcissism may be structurally interpretable as externalization primarily through dominance, with or without validation
- Psychopathic functioning may involve dominance-oriented externalization without the insecurity component characteristic of validation-seeking

These are proposed as conceptual hypotheses, not diagnostic relabelings.

### Relationship to REL

REL measures the speed of externalization. Vectors describe the direction. Together they form a complementary characterization:

- REL captures *how quickly* externalization occurs
- Vectors capture *through which channel* externalization proceeds

The framework predicts that:
- Different vectors may produce different REL profiles
- Validation-oriented ORBIT may show short REL specifically in social evaluation contexts
- Dominance-oriented ORBIT may show short REL specifically in threat/control contexts
- Shame-defense ORBIT may show extremely short REL following perceived criticism

### Relationship to Integration Theory

Integration does not eliminate vectors. It changes their function:

- **Low integration + ORBIT(validation)**: compulsive reassurance-seeking, identity collapse without feedback
- **High integration + ORBIT(validation)**: can seek validation without identity dependence, can function without it

The critical distinction remains: does coherence survive the absence of the vector's target?

### Empirical Implications

The vector model generates testable predictions:

1. Different vectors should correlate with different behavioral patterns under activation
2. Vector composition should be partially stable but context-sensitive
3. Individuals with the same overall ORBIT intensity but different vector compositions should respond differently to specific experimental paradigms (e.g., social evaluation vs. attachment ambiguity vs. control threat)

### Connection to Kowalchyk et al. (2021)

Recent research on performative self-elevation (Kowalchyk et al., 2021) is conceptually consistent with the vector distinction. Their finding that narcissism correlates with insecurity-driven self-elevation (FLEX) but not psychopathy suggests that what appears as a single construct (narcissism) may reflect different underlying regulatory strategies:

- Vulnerable narcissism may involve externalization primarily through validation-seeking, driven by insecurity
- Grandiose narcissism may involve externalization primarily through dominance, without the insecurity component

Their proposed distinction between status motivation (narcissism) and power motivation (psychopathy) is conceptually consistent with — though not identical to — the validation vs. dominance vector distinction proposed here. The degree of structural correspondence remains an empirical question.

## 4.8 Time as a Structural Variable

The framework proposes that regulation timing itself contains psychologically meaningful information. Two individuals may report similar subjective distress while differing dramatically in speed of reassurance-seeking, externalization urgency, and identity destabilization speed.

# 5. Capacity vs Behavior

## 5.1 The Critical Distinction

Many psychological models infer inner structure directly from external behavior. The CENTER/ORBIT Framework proposes that behavior alone may be insufficient for determining regulation organization. The critical question is not "How stable does the person appear?" but "How is stability being maintained?"

## 5.2 Same Behavior, Different Structure

Identical behaviors may emerge from very different underlying regulation structures. Withdrawal may reflect integrated self-regulation, avoidant suppression, shame collapse, or nervous system overload. Reassurance-seeking may reflect temporary stress activation, chronic externalized regulation, or acute destabilization.

## 5.3 Pressure Reveals Structure

The framework proposes that regulation organization becomes most visible when tension increases. When things are easy, everyone appears relational. When tension rises, regulation patterns emerge.

# 5A. Integration Theory

## 5A.1 The Problem

The CENTER/ORBIT Framework uses several related terms — integration, coherence, authenticity, self-continuity — without fully specifying their structural relationships. This section proposes an explicit integration theory within the framework.

## 5A.2 Definition

Integration refers to the capacity to maintain tension, contradiction, reality contact, and regulatory flexibility without fragmentation or defensive rigidity.

Integration is not:
- the absence of tension
- permanent stability
- emotional control
- independence from others

Integration is:
- the ability to hold contradictory experience simultaneously
- maintained contact with reality even when reality is uncomfortable
- regulatory flexibility — the capacity to shift between strategies without collapse
- structural coherence that does not require simplification of experience

## 5A.3 Integration as Composite

Integration is proposed as a composite capacity consisting of four distinguishable components:

| Component | Definition | Failure mode |
|---|---|---|
| **Tension tolerance** | Ability to hold unresolved activation without immediate discharge | Premature externalization |
| **Contradiction holding** | Ability to sustain multiple incompatible realities simultaneously | Splitting, simplification |
| **Reality contact** | Ability to perceive and remain with what is actually happening | Idealization, denial, projection |
| **Regulatory flexibility** | Ability to shift between internal and external regulation adaptively | Rigidity (locked CENTER or locked ORBIT) |

Integration emerges when all four components are sufficiently present. When any component is absent, the system compensates — often through externalization, rigidity, or fragmentation.

## 5A.4 Integration Is Not CENTER

This distinction is critical.

CENTER describes where regulation occurs (internally). Integration describes how well the system functions regardless of regulation location.

This produces four structural positions:

| | Low Integration | High Integration |
|---|---|---|
| **CENTER** | Rigid CENTER (pseudo-CENTER): internally located but inflexible, suppressive, reality-avoidant | Integrated CENTER: internally located, flexible, reality-contacting, contradiction-tolerant |
| **ORBIT** | Compulsive ORBIT: externally dependent, fragile, identity-destabilizing | Integrated ORBIT: externally supported, chosen, non-identity-dependent |

## 5A.5 Integrated ORBIT

The framework proposes that external regulation is not inherently pathological.

Integrated ORBIT refers to a state where:
- external regulation is utilized without identity dependence
- the person can function without it (but chooses to use it)
- co-regulation is mutual rather than asymmetric
- the system does not fragment upon withdrawal of external support

Examples:
- Seeking comfort from a partner during grief (without identity collapse if unavailable)
- Using therapeutic relationship for processing (without becoming dependent on therapist for coherence)
- Relying on community for meaning (without losing self when community changes)

The critical distinction is not whether external regulation occurs, but whether coherence survives its absence.

## 5A.6 Rigid CENTER (Pseudo-CENTER)

Rigid CENTER refers to internally located regulation that maintains stability through restriction rather than integration.

Characteristics:
- Low contradiction tolerance — ambiguity is eliminated rather than held
- Low reality contact — uncomfortable truths are avoided or reframed
- Low regulatory flexibility — the system cannot shift to external regulation even when appropriate
- High suppression — stability depends on not feeling rather than feeling and holding

Rigid CENTER may appear highly functional externally while remaining structurally brittle. Under sufficient attachment activation, the system may destabilize disproportionately.

## 5A.7 Compulsive ORBIT vs Adaptive Co-regulation

The framework distinguishes:

**Compulsive ORBIT:**
- Coherence collapses without external input
- Identity destabilizes upon withdrawal
- Regulation is compulsive rather than chosen
- The system cannot self-stabilize even temporarily

**Adaptive co-regulation:**
- External regulation supports but does not replace internal capacity
- The system can function independently when needed
- Co-regulation is mutual and flexible
- Identity remains continuous across relational contexts

The difference is not the presence of external regulation but the structural consequence of its absence.

## 5A.8 Relationship Between Components

The four integration components relate to existing framework constructs:

| Integration component | Related COS subdomain |
|---|---|
| Tension tolerance | Tension Retention |
| Contradiction holding | (not yet measured — proposed addition) |
| Reality contact | (not yet measured — proposed addition) |
| Regulatory flexibility | CENTER Capacity + Suppression-Control (inverse) |

This analysis reveals that the current COS scale partially measures integration but does not fully capture contradiction holding or reality contact. Future versions may require additional subdomains.

## 5A.9 Integration and REL

The framework predicts that REL is influenced by integration level:

- **High integration + short REL**: rapid but non-compulsive externalization — the person seeks connection quickly but does not destabilize without it
- **Low integration + short REL**: compulsive externalization — the person cannot maintain coherence without immediate external response
- **High integration + long REL**: genuine tension tolerance — the person holds activation while remaining emotionally accessible
- **Low integration + long REL**: defensive suppression — the person appears calm but is emotionally restricted

REL alone is therefore insufficient for determining integration level. It must be interpreted alongside suppression-control and flexibility measures.

## 5A.10 Implications for the Framework

The integration theory produces several important clarifications:

1. **CENTER is not the goal.** Integration is the goal. CENTER is one possible location for integrated regulation, but not the only one.

2. **ORBIT is not the problem.** Lack of integration is the problem. ORBIT can be integrated or pathological.

3. **The axis is not a hierarchy.** CENTER–ORBIT describes location. Integration describes quality. These are orthogonal dimensions.

4. **Therapeutic direction** is not necessarily "move toward CENTER" but "increase integration capacity regardless of current regulation location."

These clarifications reduce the risk of moral misinterpretation and align the framework more closely with clinical reality where adaptive co-regulation is healthy and necessary.

# 6. Developmental Model

## 6.1 Probabilistic Gene–Environment Interaction

The framework adopts a probabilistic developmental model. Regulation organization emerges through dynamic interaction between:
- Genetic predisposition and temperament
- Nervous system sensitivity and reward responsiveness
- Mirroring quality across significant relationships
- Co-regulation experiences
- Attachment history
- Individual-specific environmental experiences (peers, romantic partners, occupational contexts)
- Social reinforcement patterns
- Later corrective relational experiences

The framework explicitly rejects deterministic developmental interpretation and single-cause explanations.

## 6.2 Behavioral Genetic Evidence

Recent extended twin family research (Back et al., 2026) demonstrates that familial environmental factors shared by siblings play a negligible role in the development of narcissism-related traits. The parent–child correlation in narcissism is entirely genetically driven. This finding challenges developmental models that place primary emphasis on shared parenting experiences.

The framework therefore proposes that:
- Genetic factors create probabilistic predispositions toward externalized regulation (e.g., heightened reward sensitivity, attachment-system reactivity)
- Individual-specific environmental experiences shape how these predispositions develop into regulation architectures
- Mirroring processes operate across all significant relationships throughout the lifespan, not exclusively through early parental relationships
- The same genetic predisposition may produce different regulation outcomes depending on individual-specific experiences

## 6.3 Temperament as Regulation Bias

The framework proposes that individuals differ in baseline sensitivity to uncertainty, reward salience, attachment activation, social evaluation, and emotional ambiguity. These differences may influence regulation externalization speed, mirror dependence, reassurance-seeking behavior, and ORBIT susceptibility.

## 6.4 Differential Encoding

The framework proposes that individuals may encode identical relational events differently due to temperamental and neurobiological differences. Two individuals may experience the same relational environment as psychologically different regulatory realities. This may partially explain why siblings exposed to similar developmental conditions develop different regulation structures — consistent with the negligible shared environment finding in behavioral genetic research.

## 6.5 ORBIT as Externally Weighted Regulation

ORBIT is conceptualized not solely as a relational adaptation but as externally weighted regulation organization in which external signals carry unusually high regulatory salience. The framework tentatively proposes that some ORBIT-oriented systems may display heightened responsiveness to social reward, admiration, attachment reinforcement, and emotional intensity — potentially reflecting biologically influenced differences in reward processing.

## 6.6 Narcissism as Trait vs Regulation Architecture

The framework distinguishes between:
- **Narcissism as personality trait**: grandiosity, entitlement, status-seeking — substantially heritable, stable across the lifespan
- **Narcissistic regulation architecture**: externalized identity stabilization through mirroring, validation, and control — a regulation strategy that may develop through multiple pathways

The CENTER/ORBIT Framework does not claim to explain narcissism as a personality trait. It proposes to model the regulation dynamics that may accompany or underlie narcissistic functioning — specifically, the degree to which identity coherence depends on external reflective systems.

## 6.7 Assortative Mating and Relational Dynamics

Recent evidence suggests positive assortative mating in narcissism: individuals with higher narcissism tend to select partners with similar narcissism levels (Back et al., 2026). This challenges simplistic complementary models (e.g., narcissist selects submissive victim).

The framework proposes that trait-level assortative mating and functional regulation dynamics may operate at different levels of analysis:
- At the trait level, narcissistic individuals may preferentially select similar partners
- At the functional level, regulation asymmetry may still emerge within relationships regardless of trait similarity
- Two individuals with similar narcissism scores may still develop asymmetric regulation roles within a specific relational system

This distinction requires empirical investigation.

# 7. Regulation Externalization Latency (REL)

## 7.1 Definition

Regulation Externalization Latency (REL) refers to the time interval between unresolved emotional or relational activation and initiation of external regulatory behavior.

## 7.2 Theoretical Rationale

The framework proposes that unresolved tension activates competing regulatory tendencies: internal processing, reflective observation, emotional suppression, external reassurance-seeking, relational fixation, and symbolic stabilization. REL attempts to operationalize how quickly unresolved activation becomes externally regulated.

## 7.3 Distinction From Existing Constructs

| Existing Construct | Primary Focus |
|---|---|
| Distress tolerance | Ability to endure distress |
| Attachment anxiety | Fear of abandonment/rejection |
| Differentiation of self | Maintaining self-definition in emotional systems |
| Self-concept clarity | Stability and coherence of self-beliefs |
| **REL** | **Externalization timing under unresolved relational activation** |

The proposed contribution of REL is measurement of externalization timing — a dynamic process variable rather than a static trait description.

## 7.4 Proposed Experimental Paradigms

- **Delayed communication tasks**: participants receive emotionally meaningful but ambiguous communication followed by delayed response intervals
- **Attachment ambiguity conditions**: participants experience unresolved relational uncertainty without immediate clarification
- **Incomplete feedback paradigms**: participants receive ambiguous evaluative signals without closure

## 7.5 Potential Behavioral Measures

- Time to reassurance-seeking
- Message-checking frequency
- Compulsive communication attempts
- Emotional escalation timing
- Self-reported destabilization speed
- Physiological activation duration

## 7.6 Proposed Physiological Correlates

The framework predicts that shorter REL may correlate with prolonged autonomic activation, elevated stress persistence, slower physiological recovery under uncertainty, and increased attachment-system activation.

## 7.7 Distinguishing REL From Suppression

A long REL alone does not necessarily indicate high CENTER capacity. REL should always be interpreted alongside suppression-control variables, emotional accessibility measures, authenticity stability indicators, and physiological flexibility measures.

## 7.8 Temperament-Sensitive REL

REL may be partially influenced by baseline nervous system sensitivity. Short REL does not necessarily indicate weak character or pathology. Instead, short REL may emerge from interaction between biologically heightened activation sensitivity, unresolved regulation dependency, and learned externalization patterns.

# 8. Proposed Measurement Directions

The framework proposes several measurement directions for future operationalization. These are presented as exploratory starting points rather than validated instruments. Construct validation must precede scale development.

## 8.1 Measurement Philosophy

The framework assumes that regulation organization becomes most visible under conditions involving uncertainty, attachment activation, emotional ambiguity, relational instability, delayed reassurance, or identity threat. Measurement should therefore focus primarily on responses to unresolved tension rather than general personality description alone.

## 8.2 Proposed Measurement Domains

Future operationalization may investigate the following domains:

| Domain | Primary Focus | Possible approach |
|---|---|---|
| CENTER Capacity | Internal coherence under tension | Self-report + behavioral paradigm |
| External Regulation Dependence | Reliance on reassurance and relational stabilization | Self-report + ecological momentary assessment |
| Tension Retention | Ability to tolerate unresolved activation | Behavioral latency tasks |
| Authenticity Stability | Ability to remain authentic under attachment stress | Self-report + relational stress paradigm |
| Mirror Dependence | Reliance on external reflection for identity stability | Self-report + evaluative feedback paradigm |
| Self-Continuity | Stability of identity across relational contexts | Self-report + longitudinal assessment |
| Suppression-Control | Defensive inhibition vs. integrated regulation | Self-report (control variable) |

## 8.3 Example Items (Exploratory)

The following items are presented as illustrative examples for future scale development, not as a validated instrument:

**CENTER Capacity:**
- "I can tolerate uncertainty in relationships without immediately seeking reassurance."
- "Emotional tension does not automatically make me lose my sense of self."

**External Regulation Dependence:**
- "I feel emotionally unstable when communication becomes uncertain."
- "My emotional state changes rapidly depending on how others respond to me."

**Tension Retention:**
- "I can sit with emotional discomfort without needing immediate resolution."
- "Ambiguous situations do not automatically destabilize me."

**Authenticity Stability:**
- "I can express disagreement without fearing complete relational loss."
- "Relational tension does not immediately make me abandon my own needs."

**Mirror Dependence:**
- "Criticism strongly destabilizes my sense of self."
- "How others see me strongly affects how I experience myself."

**Suppression-Control (control variable):**
- "I stay emotionally stable by shutting down what I feel."
- "My stability depends on remaining emotionally controlled."

## 8.4 Validation Strategy

Future validation efforts should compare proposed constructs against established measures including attachment style inventories, intolerance of uncertainty scales, emotional regulation measures, self-concept clarity scales, rejection sensitivity measures, and differentiation of self measures.

The framework predicts partial overlap while proposing that the CENTER/ORBIT axis — particularly when measured through behavioral paradigms (REL) rather than self-report alone — may capture unique structural dimensions not fully measured by existing tools.

## 8.5 Behavioral and Physiological Extensions

Self-report measures alone may be insufficient. Future operationalization should include behavioral paradigms (delayed communication tasks, attachment ambiguity conditions), physiological measures (heart rate variability, cortisol response), and ecological momentary assessment to capture regulation dynamics in naturalistic contexts.

# 8A. Core Regulatory Model

The framework proposes the following structural architecture as its formal backbone:

## Regulatory Response Function

The following is proposed as a minimal conceptual architecture — a simplified structural model identifying the primary variables the framework considers most relevant. It is not intended as an exhaustive account of all factors influencing regulation (e.g., temperament, physiological state, reward sensitivity), but as a tractable starting point for empirical investigation.

Psychological activation generates unresolved tension (T). The regulatory response depends on:

**R = f(C, I, V, REL, S)**

where:
- **C** = CENTER capacity (maximum internal regulation competence)
- **I** = Integration level (tension tolerance + contradiction holding + reality contact + regulatory flexibility)
- **V** = Dominant ORBIT vector orientation (validation / dominance / shame-defense)
- **REL** = Regulation Externalization Latency (speed of externalization)
- **S** = Situational activation context (type and intensity of tension)

## Predicted Outcomes

| Condition | Predicted regulatory pattern |
|---|---|
| Low C + low I + short REL | Compulsive externalization (chronic ORBIT) |
| High C + high I + short REL | May reflect adaptive co-regulation if externalization remains non-compulsive |
| High C + low I + long REL | Defensive suppression (pseudo-CENTER) |
| Low C + high V dependency | Chronic vector-specific ORBIT |
| High C + high I + long REL | Integrated CENTER |

## Structural Constraints

The model proposes the following constraints:

1. **Integration is constrained by capacity, but not reducible to it**: A system cannot sustain integration beyond what its regulatory capacity can support. However, integration involves components (e.g., contradiction holding, reality contact) that are partially independent of raw distress tolerance.
2. **REL is bounded by capacity and context**: REL reflects interaction between C, I, and S — not a fixed trait.
3. **Vectors are context-sensitive**: V may shift depending on S (tension subclass).
4. **Architecture is more stable than state**: Habitual patterns (architecture level) change slower than momentary responses (state level).

## Falsifiability of the Model

The model is falsified if:
- C and I prove empirically indistinguishable (integration collapses into capacity)
- REL shows no independent predictive value beyond C and I
- Vector distinctions produce no differential behavioral predictions
- The proposed structural levels (state/strategy/architecture/capacity) cannot be empirically separated

This formal structure is proposed as a conceptual architecture, not a mathematical model. Quantitative formalization requires empirical grounding that does not yet exist.

# 9. Testable Hypotheses

## H1 — Primary Hypothesis (Revised)

Individuals with greater attachment-system sensitivity and lower internalized regulation capacity will demonstrate shorter Regulation Externalization Latency (REL) under unresolved relational ambiguity, independent of attachment anxiety, distress tolerance, emotional suppression, self-concept clarity, and differentiation of self.

## H2 — Tension Externalization

Individuals with lower CENTER capacity will externalize unresolved tension more rapidly into relational or symbolic regulation behaviors.

## H3 — Authenticity Stability

Individuals with higher CENTER capacity will maintain greater authenticity consistency during attachment activation and relational stress.

## H4 — Mirror Dependence

Higher ORBIT dependency will correlate with greater emotional fluctuation in response to external evaluative feedback.

## H5 — Ambiguity Intolerance

Individuals with higher ORBIT dependency will experience stronger destabilization during unresolved relational ambiguity.

## H6 — Symbolic Stabilization

Lower internalized regulation capacity may correlate with increased symbolic or ideological stabilization behaviors.

## H7 — Integration vs Suppression

Integrated regulation capacity will correlate more strongly with emotional flexibility, uncertainty tolerance, and authenticity stability than with emotional suppression or reduced emotional intensity.

## H8 — Relational Differentiation

Individuals with higher CENTER capacity will demonstrate greater relational differentiation during emotional closeness.

## H9 — Recovery

Individuals with higher CENTER capacity will recover more rapidly from relational disruption without requiring immediate external stabilization.

## H10 — Developmental Interaction

Regulation organization will reflect interaction between genetic predisposition (temperament, reward sensitivity) and individual-specific environmental experiences, with negligible contribution from shared familial environments — consistent with Back et al. (2026).

## H11 — Dynamic Axis

CENTER and ORBIT are predicted to fluctuate dynamically depending on stress load, attachment activation, uncertainty exposure, nervous system state, and relational stability. Even individuals with high baseline CENTER capacity may temporarily shift toward ORBIT under sufficient destabilization.

## H12 — Vector-Specific Externalization

Individuals with comparable overall ORBIT dependence but different dominant vector orientations will exhibit distinct behavioral responses under different activation contexts.

Specifically, the framework predicts that:
- Validation-oriented externalization will produce stronger behavioral response under social evaluation ambiguity (e.g., delayed feedback, ambiguous approval)
- Dominance-oriented externalization will produce stronger behavioral response under control threat (e.g., unpredictable environment, loss of authority)
- Shame-defense externalization will produce stronger behavioral response under criticism exposure (e.g., direct negative evaluation, public error)

This hypothesis is critical for establishing that vectors represent structurally distinct regulatory mechanisms rather than surface-level behavioral descriptions.

## Falsifiability

The framework assumes that its claims must remain open to disconfirmation. Potential falsifying outcomes include findings that CENTER capacity does not correlate with uncertainty tolerance, REL shows no meaningful relationship to regulation organization, mirror dependence does not relate to identity instability, or emotional regulation outcomes are unrelated to proposed CENTER/ORBIT dynamics.

# 10. Predictions

## 10.1 Uncertainty Tolerance

Higher CENTER capacity will correlate with longer tolerance of unresolved uncertainty without requiring immediate external stabilization.

## 10.2 Relationship Stability

Relationships involving greater internalized regulation capacity may produce lower initial intensity but greater long-term coherence. Emotional intensity alone will not reliably predict relational stability.

## 10.3 Limerence and Idealization

Unresolved internal fragmentation may increase susceptibility to limerence, idealization, and sacred object formation. Idealized external objects may function as temporary coherence stabilizers for externally regulated systems.

## 10.4 Distinguishing Suppression From Integration

Emotional suppression and integrated regulation will produce measurably different outcomes. Emotional flatness alone will not reliably indicate integrated regulation capacity.

## 10.5 Cultural Variation

CENTER and ORBIT may manifest differently across cultural contexts while preserving similar underlying regulation dynamics. Cross-cultural validation is essential.

## 10.6 Therapeutic Change

Successful therapy may involve gradual internalization of regulation previously dependent on external stabilization, measurable through increased tension tolerance, reduced compulsive reassurance-seeking, and improved authenticity stability.

## 10.7 Gene–Environment Interaction

The combination of genetically based disposition toward externalized regulation (e.g., strong reactivity to novel rewards) and certain repeated individual-specific experiences (e.g., status boosts in peer, romantic, and occupational contexts) may be particularly strong drivers of regulation organization trajectories — consistent with proposed GWAS and LEWAS approaches (Back et al., 2026).

# 11. Limits of the Model

## 11.1 The Framework Is Preliminary

The CENTER/ORBIT Framework is currently conceptual rather than empirically established. Constructs have not been psychometrically validated, proposed measures remain experimental, predictive claims remain untested, and causal assumptions remain hypothetical.

## 11.2 Overlap With Existing Theories

Many concepts overlap substantially with attachment theory, affect regulation theory, self psychology, object relations theory, and differentiation of self theory. The framework must demonstrate that its constructs provide additional predictive value or improved structural clarity beyond re-labeling existing ideas.

## 11.3 Risk of Overgeneralization

The framework attempts to describe a broad range of phenomena through regulation organization. Many human experiences may involve factors insufficiently captured by regulation-based models alone, including culture, cognition, temperament, biology, socioeconomic conditions, trauma, and neurodiversity.

## 11.4 Difficulty Distinguishing Integration From Suppression

One of the framework's major unresolved methodological problems involves differentiating genuine integration from defensive suppression, emotional inhibition, or avoidant adaptation. Behavioral calmness alone may conceal radically different underlying regulation structures.

## 11.5 State vs Capacity: Empirical Separability

The framework proposes a four-level structural hierarchy (state, strategy, architecture, capacity). However, the empirical separability of these proposed levels remains untested. Important unresolved questions include whether these levels can be reliably distinguished through measurement, whether they show different temporal dynamics, and whether interventions differentially target specific levels. Longitudinal and multi-method research is required to validate this hierarchy.

## 11.6 Measurement Challenges

Operationalizing regulation organization presents substantial methodological difficulties including self-report bias, defensive self-presentation, social desirability effects, and situational variability. The framework may require behavioral paradigms, physiological measures, and ecological assessment rather than relying solely on self-report.

## 11.7 Cultural Limitations

The framework may contain implicit cultural assumptions regarding individuality, autonomy, and emotional expression. Some behaviors interpreted as ORBIT-oriented in one cultural context may represent normative relational functioning in another.

## 11.8 Risk of Moral Misinterpretation

Although the framework attempts to remain structural rather than moral, CENTER may be socially interpreted as superior and ORBIT as pathological. The framework explicitly rejects these interpretations.

## 11.9 Neurobiological Speculation

The framework currently lacks direct empirical evidence linking proposed regulation organizations to specific neurobiological mechanisms. All neurobiological interpretations remain provisional and speculative.

## 11.10 Normative Assumption

Whether internalized regulation provides adaptive advantage across contexts remains context-dependent and requires empirical investigation. The framework acknowledges that high ORBIT orientation may be adaptive in collectivistic cultures, high-coordination environments, or situations requiring rapid social responsiveness. The CENTER–ORBIT distinction describes regulatory location, not universal adaptive value.

## 11.11 Primary Limitation

The model currently possesses greater conceptual coherence than empirical validation. Bridging this gap represents the framework's central future challenge.

# 12. Future Research

## 12.1 Construct Validation

The most immediate priority is validation of primary constructs through factor analysis, internal consistency testing, test–retest reliability, and discriminant validity studies.

## 12.2 REL Measurement

Development of behavioral paradigms measuring Regulation Externalization Latency under controlled conditions of relational ambiguity.

## 12.3 Longitudinal Research

Investigation of stability of CENTER capacity across development, effects of attachment disruption, and whether internalized regulation capacity can meaningfully increase through corrective relational experience.

## 12.4 Physiological Correlates

Investigation of relationships between regulation organization and heart rate variability, stress hormone response, autonomic recovery patterns, and physiological response to attachment ambiguity.

## 12.5 Gene–Environment Interaction

Investigation of how genetic predisposition toward reward sensitivity and approach motivation interacts with individual-specific environmental experiences to produce regulation organization — potentially through combined GWAS and LEWAS approaches as proposed by Back et al. (2026).

## 12.6 Cross-Cultural Validation

Essential investigation of how CENTER and ORBIT dynamics manifest across different cultural contexts.

## 12.7 Clinical Research

Investigation of potential relevance to personality organization, attachment disturbance, emotional dysregulation, and identity instability — while acknowledging that the framework is not currently intended for clinical diagnosis.

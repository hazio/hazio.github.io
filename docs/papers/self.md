# Self Architecture

## Core idea

**Self Architecture** refers to the structural organization of the self: how self-beliefs, self-worth, identity continuity, shame, dependency, and self–other boundaries are maintained under psychological activation.

It is not a single established construct in psychology. Rather, it is a proposed integrative layer built from existing research on self-concept clarity, contingent self-worth, shame, attachment, mentalization, and self-coherence.

## Scientific anchors

Self-research already distinguishes between the **content** of the self-concept and its **structure**. Self-concept clarity, for example, refers to how clearly, confidently, consistently, and stably self-beliefs are defined. This supports the idea that the self has architecture, not merely content. 

Contingent self-worth research shows that self-esteem can depend on specific domains such as achievement, approval, appearance, or status. This supports the idea that self-worth is not simply global, but condition-dependent.

Shame research supports the idea that self-evaluative emotions can threaten the organization of the self rather than merely produce unpleasant affect.

Mentalization and attachment traditions further suggest that self-experience is shaped by the capacity to represent one’s own and others’ mental states, especially under relational activation.

## Proposed Self Architecture dimensions

### 1. Self-Coherence

**Question:** Can the self remain integrated under contradiction?

High self-coherence:

> “I made a mistake, but I remain myself.”

Low self-coherence:

> “This mistake invalidates me.”

Self-coherence concerns whether conflicting self-relevant information can be integrated without collapse.

### 2. Self-Concept Clarity

**Question:** Are self-beliefs clear, internally consistent, and stable?

This overlaps with the established construct of self-concept clarity.

Low clarity may appear as unstable identity, contradictory self-beliefs, or difficulty describing oneself consistently.

### 3. Worth Contingency

**Question:** What must remain true for me to feel worthy?

Examples of worth-contingent domains:

- admiration
- competence
- achievement
- morality
- attractiveness
- attachment
- status
- autonomy

High worth contingency means self-worth fluctuates strongly when a key domain is threatened.

### 4. Shame Integration Capacity

**Question:** Can shame be experienced without identity collapse?

High shame integration:

> “I feel exposed and regretful, but I can repair.”

Low shame integration:

> “I am defective, humiliated, or annihilated.”

This distinguishes shame as an affect from the self’s ability to metabolize shame.

### 5. Dependency Tolerance

**Question:** Can conscious dependency be survived?

High dependency tolerance:

> “I need others sometimes, and this does not destroy me.”

Low dependency tolerance:

> “Needing others means humiliation, weakness, or loss of self.”

This is especially relevant to narcissistic and avoidant structures.

### 6. Self–Other Differentiation

**Question:** Can self and other remain distinct without collapse?

High differentiation:

> “You are separate from me, and I remain myself.”

Low differentiation:

> “Your autonomy threatens my coherence.”

This dimension connects to boundaries, individuation, fusion, and separateness distress.

### 7. Ontological Self-Continuity

**Question:** Can I remain experientially real without external recognition?

High ontological continuity:

> “I exist even when I am unseen.”

Low ontological continuity:

> “If I am not seen, I disappear.”

This is speculative but clinically meaningful. It may relate to fragmentation anxiety, annihilation anxiety, or severe self-coherence instability.

## Relationship to Compression Profiles

Compression Profile describes:

> how reality is processed.

Self Architecture describes:

> what reality threatens.

For example, a person may have low shame resolution because shame threatens self-coherence. The compression is not random; it protects a vulnerable self-structure.

## Relationship to SRF

Self Architecture answers the question:

> what must be protected for the system to remain coherent?

SRF explains the regulatory mechanism:

- IRC: how much activation can be held
- SF: how flexibly the signal can be processed
- ERD: how much external stabilization is needed
- REL: how costly full-resolution exposure is
- EV: what external vector is activated

## Narcissistic example

A narcissistic self architecture may include:

- fragile self-coherence
- high worth contingency
- low shame integration
- low dependency tolerance
- weak self–other differentiation
- possible low ontological self-continuity
- reliance on grandiosity or external mirroring for stabilization

This does not mean every narcissistic presentation has the same architecture. It means these dimensions may help describe why certain signals become destabilizing.

## Core formulation

Self Architecture is the structural organization of the self that determines what kinds of reality can be integrated without collapse.

Or more simply:

> Compression shows where perception narrows.
> Self Architecture shows why narrowing is necessary.
